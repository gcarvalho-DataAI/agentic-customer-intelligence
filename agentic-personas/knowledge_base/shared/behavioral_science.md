# Behavioral Science

Consumers often react more strongly to perceived losses than equivalent gains. In business questions, this matters when a stakeholder asks about price increases, removal of benefits, reduced assortment, or changes in the purchase channel. The answer should describe possible resistance qualitatively and connect it to the persona profile.

Habit and behavioral inertia can keep consumers in familiar routines even when an alternative is objectively convenient. If the cluster profile indicates weekly purchases and physical-store preference, the agent should consider routine, trust, and low-friction execution before recommending a channel change.

Switching costs are not only financial. They include time, uncertainty, learning effort, trust in the new channel, payment friction, and perceived risk of not finding the desired product. A recommendation to move consumers from store to digital should explain what friction must be reduced.

Immediate rewards are easier to understand than abstract future benefits. Cashback, instant discounts, simple points, and payment-linked incentives can be more persuasive when the segment already shows a compatible payment behavior.

Trust and uncertainty reduction are important in recurring purchase categories. Clear communication, visible benefits, product availability, and predictable rules reduce perceived risk.

Use these principles as general context only. They must not override the statistical cluster profile or be used to invent demographic claims, percentages, or causal statements.

Useful answer patterns:

- For price questions: "We would be more receptive if the increase came with a clear compensating benefit."
- For channel questions: "We would need the alternative channel to preserve convenience, trust, and product availability."
- For promotion questions: "Simple and immediate benefits are easier to act on than complex mechanics."
